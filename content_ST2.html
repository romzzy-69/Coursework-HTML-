Ocean Pollution & Plastic Waste

In this article

  1. What is ocean pollution?
  2. Plastic waste and microplastics
  3. Oil spills and chemical waste
  4. Effects on marine animals
  5. What students and communities can do

1. What is ocean pollution?

Ocean pollution is the introduction of toxic materials and harmful pollutants such as agricultural and industrial waste, chemicals, oil spills, and plastic waste into the ocean waters.

  • Agricultural runoff carrying fertilisers and pesticides
  • Industrial discharge of chemicals and heavy metals
  • Plastic litter carried into the sea via rivers

Over 8 million tonnes of plastic enter the ocean every year — equivalent to dumping a rubbish truck full of plastic into the sea every minute.

2. Plastic waste and microplastics

Plastics do not fully degrade. Instead they break down into smaller pieces called microplastics, which are ingested by marine life and enter the food chain.

  • Plastic takes 400 to 1,000 years to break down
  • Microplastics have been found in human blood and lungs
  • Marine animals mistake plastic bags for jellyfish

By 2050 there will be more plastic in the ocean by weight than fish if current trends continue.

Figure: Locals Fishing on a beach (Ref: Royalty-free image placeholder)

3. Oil spills and chemical waste

Accidental oil spills from tankers and offshore drilling severely impact marine birds, mammals, and fish.

  • Suffocates fish by coating their gills
  • Destroys coral reefs and seagrass beds
  • Contaminates food supply for coastal communities

The 1989 Exxon Valdez spill released 11 million gallons of oil, killing 250,000 seabirds and billions of fish eggs.

Figure: Dark ocean waves representing polluted waters (Ref: Royalty-free image placeholder)

4. Effects on marine animals

Animals can become entangled in debris or mistake plastic for food, causing starvation, suffocation, and death.

  • Sea turtles mistake plastic bags for jellyfish
  • Seabirds feed plastic fragments to their chicks
  • Whales become entangled in abandoned fishing gear

Over 700 marine species are known to be affected by plastic pollution and 100,000 marine mammals die from entanglement every year.

Figure: Sea turtle swimming, vulnerable to plastic bags (Ref: Royalty-free image placeholder)

5. What students and communities can do

Reduce single-use plastics, participate in beach cleanups, recycle correctly, and support legislation against ocean dumping.

  • Switch to a reusable water bottle and coffee cup
  • Join or organise a local beach cleanup
  • Spread awareness on social media using facts

If every person in the UK gave up just one single-use plastic item per day it would prevent over 66 billion plastic items from entering the waste stream each year.
