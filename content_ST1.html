Coral Reefs & Coastal Ecosystems

In this article

  1. What are coral reefs?
  2. Why coral reefs matter
  3. Threats to coral reefs
  4. Sri Lankan coral reefs
  5. Conservation efforts

1. What are coral reefs?

Coral reefs are large underwater structures composed of the skeletons of colonial marine invertebrates called coral. The coral species that build reefs are known as hermatypic, or "hard," corals.

Figure: Close up of vibrant coral reef (Ref: Royalty-free image placeholder)

2. Why coral reefs matter

They protect coastlines from storms and erosion, provide jobs for local communities, and offer opportunities for recreation. They are also a source of food and new medicines.

Figure: Sea turtle swimming among reefs showing biodiversity (Ref: Royalty-free image placeholder)

3. Threats to coral reefs

Pollution, overfishing, destructive fishing practices using dynamite or cyanide, collecting live corals for the aquarium market, and mining coral for building materials are some of the many ways people damage reefs all around the world.

4. Sri Lankan coral reefs (Hikkaduwa & Pigeon Island)

Sri Lanka has fringing reefs along most of its coastline. Hikkaduwa National Park is one of the two marine national parks in Sri Lanka. Pigeon Island National Park is known for its beautiful coral reefs and sharks.

Figure: Sri lankan coastline with clear water (Ref: Royalty-free image placeholder)

5. Conservation efforts

Marine protected areas, coral restoration projects, sustainable tourism, and pollution reduction campaigns are vital for saving these ecosystems.

Figure: Volunteers cleaning the beach to protect reefs (Ref: Royalty-free image placeholder)
