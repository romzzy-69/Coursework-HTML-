Coral Reefs Guide

1. What are coral reefs?

Coral reefs are large underwater structures built by colonies of tiny marine animals. These "hard" corals create limestone skeletons that form vibrant, bustling ocean habitats.

  • Made by thousands of tiny soft-bodied animals called polyps
  • Take thousands of years to grow into complex structures
  • Cover just 1% of the ocean but house 25% of all marine life

Known as the "rainforests of the sea," coral reefs support over a million different aquatic species.

2. Why coral reefs matter

Reefs are lifelines for both the ocean and humans. They shield coastlines from rough weather and provide essential food, jobs, and even medicines for people worldwide.

  • Block up to 97% of wave energy, stopping coastal erosion
  • Act as safe nurseries for commercial and local fish
  • Provide natural ingredients for life-saving medicines

Reefs contribute $375 billion to the global economy each year, supporting the food security and jobs of half a billion people.

Figure: Coral reef ecosystem with tropical fish (Ref: Royalty-free image placeholder)

3. Threats to coral reefs

Human activities are destroying these fragile ecosystems at an alarming rate. Pollution, aggressive fishing, and coastal development are putting immense stress on reefs.

  • Fishing with destructive dynamite or toxic cyanide
  • Water pollution from farming runoff and plastic waste
  • Mining corals for construction and the aquarium trade

We have already lost 50% of the world’s coral reefs. Without urgent action, 90% could disappear by 2050.

4. Sri Lankan coral reefs (Hikkaduwa & Pigeon Island)

Sri Lanka’s coastline is rich with beautiful fringing reefs. Hikkaduwa is loved for its shallow, accessible coral gardens, while Pigeon Island is famous for its reef sharks.

  • Hikkaduwa is one of the island's first marine sanctuaries
  • Pigeon Island hosts over 100 coral and 300 fish species
  • Both are safe havens for reef sharks and endangered sea turtles

These reefs are the backbone of Sri Lanka’s coastal economy, supporting local fishing families and thriving eco-tourism.

Figure: Sri lankan coastline with clear water (Ref: Royalty-free image placeholder)

5. Conservation efforts

We can save our reefs through active, targeted protection. Combining marine policies with local community action gives these vital ecosystems a real chance to heal.

  • Creating Marine Protected Areas (MPAs) to stop overfishing
  • Replanting healthy corals through active restoration projects
  • Reducing plastic waste and choosing sustainable tourism

You can help too! Simply switching to reef-safe sunscreen and refusing single-use plastics makes a direct, positive impact.
